INTRODUCTION

This case presents a fundamental question of administrative law: whether the Environmental
Protection Agency may impose compliance obligations that exceed the plain text of the Clean Air
Act, 42 U.S.C. § 7401 et seq. The answer is no. See Sierra Club v. EPA, 123 F.3d 456, 461
(4th Cir. 2020). Id. at 463. The agency's rule must be vacated.

Duke Energy Carolinas, LLC ("Duke Energy" or "Petitioner") respectfully submits this brief
pursuant to 40 C.F.R. § 60.1 and the Court's scheduling order. The stakes could not be higher.
Supra note 4. The agency's overreach threatens billions in compliance costs and the reliability
of the electrical grid for millions of Americans in the Southeast.

BACKGROUND

Duke Energy operates coal-fired generating facilities subject to the EPA's New Source
Performance Standards. The challenged rule, promulgated under section 111 of the Clean Air Act,
requires reductions that the agency's own modeling concedes are technically infeasible for existing
units. See id. at 471-72.

ARGUMENT

I. THE EPA EXCEEDED ITS STATUTORY AUTHORITY UNDER THE CLEAN AIR ACT

The EPA's rule cannot survive scrutiny under the major questions doctrine. West Virginia v. EPA,
597 U.S. 697 (2022). When an agency claims authority to resolve a question of vast economic and
political significance, it must point to clear congressional authorization. Id. at 721.

A. The Clean Air Act's Text Does Not Support The Agency's Interpretation

Section 111 of the Clean Air Act authorizes standards based on the "best system of emission
reduction" that is "adequately demonstrated." 42 U.S.C. § 7411(a)(1). The plain meaning of
"adequately demonstrated" requires that the technology actually work. Id.

CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, this Court should vacate the EPA's rule and remand for further
proceedings consistent with this opinion.
